## Introduction
In today's increasingly complex software systems, we often encounter this pain point: business logic is torn apart by frequently changing product requirements, and the code structure gradually evolves into a difficult-to-maintain "big mud ball."

From the initial simple traditional monolithic architecture to today's complex distributed and microservice architectures, the evolution of software architecture has gone through multiple stages. How can we gracefully divide service boundaries and control business complexity in an era of high concurrency and prevalent microservices? **DDD (Domain-Driven Design)** has become the ultimate weapon to break the deadlock.

## The Evolution of Software Architecture
To better understand the value of DDD, let's first look at the three typical stages of software architecture evolution:

* **Traditional Monolithic Architecture**: All application functions are integrated into a single application, with all modules and components running in the same process. Requests directly operate on the database, sometimes without even code layering. Although easy for early development and deployment, as the business grows, modules with different functions become tightly coupled, and a change in one place affects the whole system.
* **MVC Layered Architecture**: The application is divided into different layers (e.g., controller, service, mapper). Layers interact through interfaces, promoting modularity. However, when facing complex business scenarios, the flexibility and scalability of MVC architecture are limited. As the business grows, the service layer becomes extremely bloated, and maintenance costs gradually increase.
* **Microservice Architecture**: The system is split into multiple small, independent services, each developed, deployed, and maintained by an independent team, communicating through lightweight protocols (e.g., HTTP, RPC, or message queues). Services are independent and easy to scale horizontally.

### 🚨 The "Art" Challenge of Microservice Decomposition
With the popularity of microservices, the industry faces a new challenge: **How granular should service decomposition be? How should boundaries be defined?**
* **Too fine-grained decomposition**: Project complexity is too high, with increased costs for interface network calls, distributed transaction processing, and service operations.
* **Too coarse-grained decomposition**: Business boundaries become blurred, services remain highly coupled, and the advantages of microservices are lost.

**DDD is a methodology that guides us to determine business boundaries based on domain models**, thereby dividing application boundaries and ultimately implementing service boundaries and code boundaries.

## Core Concepts of DDD Domain-Driven Design
DDD defines domain models through domain-driven design methods, thereby determining business and application boundaries, ensuring consistency between business models and code models.

### 1. Three Goals of DDD
* **Implement business requirements through domain models**: Enable developers and domain experts to jointly understand business requirements, form a shared language, and build models.
* **Improve system flexibility and maintainability**: By reasonably dividing bounded contexts, reduce system coupling, allowing different modules to evolve independently.
* **Support expression of complex business logic**: Through in-depth business modeling, allow complex business logic to be clearly and accurately reflected in the code.

> **Core Idea**: Make the system more aligned with the business, making large systems easier to build and maintain independently.

### 2. Two Construction Phases of DDD
DDD construction is a combination of moves, usually including **Strategic Design** and **Tactical Design**.

#### ① Strategic Design (Business-Oriented: Defining Boundaries)
Starting from the business, establish domain models and unify bounded contexts. Design usually involves **Event Storming**:
* **Scene Restoration**: Domain experts, architects, developers, testers, product managers, and project managers gather together, covering a whiteboard with colorful sticky notes, brainstorming.
* **Core Discussion**: What business processes does the system involve? Which business action triggers another? What are the inputs and outputs?
* **Converging Boundaries**: Sort out the relationships between domain objects, cluster them to form aggregates, aggregate roots, and bounded contexts, ultimately completing the decomposition of microservices.

In event storming, product design methods are often combined, such as **Use Case Analysis** (describing system interactions with the outside), **Scenario Analysis** (exploring user usage in different environments), and **User Journey Analysis** (depicting a series of steps from start to finish).

#### ② Tactical Design (Technology-Oriented: Code Implementation)
Starting from technical implementation, map domain models to code models. This phase is mainly responsible for code implementation, including the design and implementation of aggregates, aggregate roots, entities, value objects, and other code logic.

## Hardcore Explanation of DDD Terminology

To avoid "talking past each other" in actual coding, we need to thoroughly understand the core terms of DDD:

| Core Term | One-Sentence Definition | Core Features and Code Mapping |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Domain** | The problem space and boundary of business concern. | Used to determine scope and boundaries. Can be further divided into Core Domain (core competitiveness), Generic Domain (e.g., payment), and Supporting Domain (e.g., gateway). |
| **Bounded Context** | The business semantic environment of a unified ubiquitous language. | Defines business boundaries. For example, in e-commerce semantics it's called "Product", in transportation semantics it's called "Cargo"; the context ensures no ambiguity within the team. |
| **Entity** | A business object with a unique identifier (ID). | State can change but ID remains unchanged. In code, it usually adopts the **Rich Domain Model** (business logic directly written in the entity class). |
| **Value Object** | An object without a unique identifier, only describing characteristics. | State is immutable; modification can only be done by **complete replacement**. For example, a user's "address" attribute. |
| **Aggregate** | A combination of multiple entities and value objects into a whole. | A high-cohesion, low-coupling organization. It is the **basic unit** for data modification and persistence, and the smallest unit for microservice decomposition. |
| **Aggregate Root** | The leader within an aggregate, the unified external interface. | The aggregate root is also an entity with an ID. External parties must not directly access other entities within the aggregate; they must cooperate through the interface provided by the aggregate root. |
| **Domain Service** | Cross-object business behavior that cannot be attributed to a single entity. | Encapsulates core business rules. For example, the "order payment" logic involving interactions among multiple entities like order, account, and payment. |

> 💡 **Viewing the World from a Dynamic Perspective**: Entities and value objects are not fixed. For a computer host, a graphics card is a value object (replace it if broken); but for a graphics card manufacturer, the graphics card is an entity with a factory serial number that needs full tracking.

## DDD Architecture Design: Rich Domain Model vs. Anemic Domain Model

When implementing tactical design, how to organize the data and behavior of domain objects is the watershed that distinguishes traditional development from DDD development. This essentially reflects different understandings of the "division of responsibilities between data and behavior" in domain objects, mainly divided into **Anemic Domain Model** and **Rich Domain Model**.

| Dimension | Anemic Domain Model | Rich Domain Model |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Definition** | Domain objects only contain data (attributes) and getters/setters, without business logic. | Domain objects contain not only data but also the business logic that processes that data. |
| **Object Role** | Pure "data container", only state, no behavior. | True "object-oriented", both state and behavior. |
| **Logic Ownership** | All business logic is stripped out into separate service classes (e.g., Service layer). | Business logic belonging to the object itself is directly cohesive within the object. |
| **Applicable Scenarios** | Suitable for simple business (e.g., traditional CRUD systems). | Suitable for complex business, tightly coupling business logic and data. |

## 4. Evolution of DDD Classic Four-Layer Architecture

After understanding the rich domain model, how do we build a stage for these vibrant domain objects? This is DDD's **four-layer layered architecture model**. It divides the application into four layers with clear responsibilities, promoting high cohesion, low coupling, and high maintainability of the code.

---

### 1. Hardcore Breakdown of DDD Four-Layer Architecture

#### ① User Interface Layer
* **Positioning**: Also called the presentation layer or Web layer, mainly responsible for interacting with the outside (users, APIs of other systems, front-end pages).
* **Responsibilities**: Receive user input and return system output. **This layer must not contain any business logic**. It is mainly responsible for parameter validation (JSR303), authentication, and forwarding user request data (DTO) to the application layer.

#### ② Application Layer
* **Positioning**: The "diplomat" and "director" of the entire system, mainly used for **coordination and orchestration**.
* **Responsibilities**: The application layer itself does not contain any core business rules. It implements specific business use cases by calling entities/domain services from the domain layer and resources from the infrastructure layer (e.g., sending emails, MQ messages). Additionally, **distributed transaction control (`@Transactional`), security permission checks, and external remote RPC calls** are also placed in this layer.

#### ③ Domain Layer
* **Positioning**: **The absolute core and soul of the entire architecture**, purely cohesive business logic.
* **Responsibilities**: Contains the core business rules, strategies, and state transitions of the application. The previously mentioned aggregate roots, entities, value objects, and domain services all reside here. Its design is completely decoupled from underlying technologies (e.g., databases, caches), aiming only to accurately translate real business requirements into code.

#### ④ Infrastructure Layer
* **Positioning**: Provides underlying technical support and common services for the upper layers.
* **Responsibilities**: Responsible for physical interaction with external systems (e.g., MySQL, Redis, RabbitMQ). Provides data persistence implementation, logging, email sending, and other tools.

---

### 2. Strict Layering vs. Relaxed Layering

In the call relationships of layered architecture, there are usually two schools in the industry:

* **Strict Layering Architecture**: Each layer can only depend on the **immediate lower layer**. That is: `User Interface Layer` -> `Application Layer` -> `Domain Layer` -> `Infrastructure Layer`. This structure is the most robust, with each layer being a natural sandbox, achieving the most thorough decoupling.
* **Relaxed Layering Architecture**: Interactions between layers are more flexible. For example, the user interface layer or application layer can bypass the domain layer and directly call the infrastructure layer to read underlying report data. This approach is very efficient in rapid development and pure query (CQRS) scenarios, but as system complexity increases, it can easily lead to dependency confusion.

> **Byolio Pitfall Avoidance Guide (Dependency Inversion Principle DIP)**:
> Traditional development habits have upper layers depending on lower layers, causing the domain layer to be tightly bound to the database.
> DDD's infrastructure layer adopts the **Dependency Inversion Principle**. The domain layer only defines **interface specifications** for persistence (e.g., `OrderRepository` interface), while the infrastructure layer is responsible for writing the **concrete implementation** of this interface (e.g., `OrderRepositoryImpl`).
> This way, the entire core domain layer becomes a **pure Java module that does not depend on any specific framework**. Even if MySQL is later replaced with MongoDB, only the infrastructure layer implementation needs to be rewritten, while the core business logic remains rock solid.

### 3. Transformation from Traditional Three-Layer Architecture to DDD Four-Layer Architecture

For students familiar with SpringBoot development, we are most accustomed to the three-layer architecture (Controller-Service-Dao). The following diagram and code structure intuitively show how they map and evolve into the DDD four-layer architecture:

#### 📐 Responsibility and Concept Mapping Comparison

| Traditional Three-Layer Architecture (MVC) | DDD Four-Layer Architecture | Core Transformation Logic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Controller Layer** (Presentation Layer) | **User Interface Layer** | Responsibilities are basically aligned, responsible for HTTP request handling, DTO validation, and response assembly. |
| **Service Layer** (Business Logic Layer) | **Application Layer + Domain Layer** | **Core split point!** The traditional all-purpose Service is split into two: process orchestration and third-party integration go to `Application`, while core business rules settle into `Domain` entities and domain services. |
| **DAO / Repository Layer** | **Infrastructure Layer** | Traditional Dao only writes SQL. DDD's infrastructure layer not only handles the database (implementing the Repository interface defined by Domain) but also takes over all technical details like Redis, MQ, etc. |

#### 📁 Package Structure Evolution in SpringBoot

```text
// Traditional Three-Layer MVC Architecture
com.byolio.project
├── controller     // Various Controllers
├── service        // Bloated business logic, procedural
│   └── impl
├── model          // entity objects / vo objects / dto objects
└── mapper         // MyBatis Mapper

---------------- Evolves into ----------------

// DDD Four-Layer Architecture (Monolithic Module Package Structure Example)
com.byolio.project
├── interfaces             // 1. User Interface Layer
│   ├── assembler     // DTO converter, responsible for converting request parameters into domain layer entities.
│   ├── controller     // Controller layer
│   ├── vo             // vo objects
│   └── dto          // Data Transfer Objects, responsible for request parameter validation and response assembly.
|
├── application    // 2. Application Layer (very thin, only responsible for assembly and orchestration)
│   └── service    // Cross-domain business logic
|
├── domain         // 3. Domain Layer (core, pure Java, does not depend on specific technical frameworks)
│   ├── order      // Divide bounded contexts by aggregate
│   │   ├── entity      // Rich domain model entity (aggregate root)
│   │   ├── valobject    // Value objects
│   │   ├── repository  // Pure interface definition! No implementation
│   │   └── service     // Domain services
|
├── infrastructure // 4. Infrastructure Layer (concrete technical implementations)
│   ├── config    // Configuration classes
│   ├── common     // Common components
│   ├── api        // API interfaces
│   ├── mapper     // Database mapper layer
│   ├── manager    // Infrastructure common components
│   ├── utils      // Utility classes
│   └── repository // Core! Implements the Repository interface defined by the domain layer
```

> **About the Introduction of shared (Shared Domain)**:
> Many students, when first writing DDD, fall into a rigid principle—since strong isolation is required, each aggregate must be completely independent. This leads to objects like Money (amount value object) and BaseEntity (base class with common ID and time fields) being repeated three or four times across packages like order, user, and product.
> The shared package is born to break this "rigid isolation." It stores low-level, universally agreed-upon domain concepts that have no business ambiguity across the entire system.
> However, be wary of shared becoming a new big mud ball! Only objects without specific business specificity (e.g., common exception definitions, purely generic value objects) should be placed in shared. If part of OrderEntity is also extracted into shared for convenience and directly depended upon by other aggregates, it completely destroys the boundaries of bounded contexts, leading to total failure. Remember: It's better to have moderate duplication than to blindly share business logic.


## Disadvantages of DDD
Although DDD can perfectly handle complex business, it is by no means a silver bullet. In actual implementation, it also has undeniable pain points:

1. **Extremely Steep Learning Curve**
   DDD introduces a large number of abstract concepts (e.g., bounded contexts, aggregate roots, rich domain models, value objects). Team members need to completely reverse the traditional "CRUD / database table-driven" thinking pattern, and the cost of mindset transformation is extremely high.
2. **Pain Point of "One Change Affects Everything" in Field Modification**
   In traditional "anemic model + database-driven" development, if a new field needs to be added to a table structure, you often only need to modify the underlying Entity and the top-level DTO, and the data can be "transparently passed through."
   However, under DDD's strict layering and anti-corruption mechanisms, due to the introduction of a large number of object isolations, **adding just one field to the underlying table structure may require synchronously modifying 4 or 5 object definitions and corresponding Mapper converters**. This extremely high cost of change can be very frustrating in the early stages of agile development with frequent changes.
3. **Easily Becomes a Formality, Hollowing Out the Domain Layer (Formalistic DDD)**
   This is the most common "derailment" scenario when domestic teams implement DDD. Due to developers' deep-rooted habits of "procedural thinking" and "Service mania," when actually coding, they often prioritize speed and write all business logic directly in the top-level Application Service, while the lower-level Domain entities and domain services become useless "messengers" and empty shells.
   This formalistic DDD of "four-layer architecture on the outside, still anemic on the inside" not only fails to enjoy the benefits of high object cohesion but also adds several layers of code call overhead, becoming a true "architectural burden."

## FAQ
### Why do different companies have different DDD architectures?
This is the most common confusion for students who have just read technical blogs and then look at enterprise-level source code in their companies. The answer is simple: **DDD is a design methodology, not a rigid code framework.**

The specific code architecture that is implemented often depends on trade-offs and compromises across the following three core dimensions:

#### 1. Team Compromise Between "Strict Layering" and "Engineering Efficiency"
* **Strict Layering**: In some very large financial, payment, and core e-commerce teams, they strictly adhere to isolation principles. The UI layer and domain layer may even be in different **Maven Multi-Module** structures. To prevent any contamination, they prefer to write a large number of `Converter`s, with a single field being transferred 4 or 5 times.
* **Engineering Efficiency**: Many small and medium-sized teams or fast-iterating businesses, for "engineering efficiency," adopt **package isolation within a single module**. They may even allow the `interfaces` layer to bypass the domain layer and directly reach the underlying `mapper` in query (CQRS) scenarios. They know that if changing a field requires 5 transfers every day, team development efficiency will be directly dragged down.

#### 2. Different Lifecycle Stages of "Monolith" vs. "Microservices"
* **Monolithic Evolution Stage**: If the system is still a large monolith, when dividing package structures, a `shared` package is often set up. Because within the same process, sharing some basic assets can greatly reduce duplicate code.
* **Microservice Maturity Stage**: If the team has already split each aggregate into physically isolated microservices, they will basically remove the large, all-encompassing `shared` package. Because microservices emphasize **complete decoupling and zero dependency**. Even if two microservices have 80% similar `Money` value objects, they would rather copy one in each service to gain the ability to deploy and evolve independently.

#### 3. Influence of Conway's Law: Organizational Structure Determines Code Architecture
> "Organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations." — Conway's Law

Each company's business complexity and even team personnel structure are different:
* Some companies have extremely complex and frequently changing businesses (e.g., complex supply chain systems), so they design the `domain` layer to be very thick and heavy, heavily guarded.
* Some companies, although claiming to implement DDD, actually have 90% of their business as statistical reports and CRUD operations. Their `domain` layer naturally becomes an empty shell, and the entire architecture leans more towards the traditional anemic model, just wearing a four-layer skin.

## Summary
DDD is not a specific framework but a design philosophy that brings technology back to the essence of business. Through strategic design, we can clarify complex business boundaries at a macro level; through tactical design, we can use tools like entities and aggregate roots to write more expressive and robust code at a micro level.

When facing long-cycle, cross-departmental collaboration, and complex long-term maintenance projects, DDD can endow the system with strong vitality and evolution capabilities.
