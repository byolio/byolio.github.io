## Introduction
In today's increasingly complex software systems, we often encounter this pain point: business logic is fragmented by frequently changing product requirements, and the code structure gradually evolves into an unmaintainable "big ball of mud."

From the initial simple traditional monolithic architecture to today's complex distributed and microservices architectures, the evolution of software architecture has gone through multiple stages. How can we elegantly delineate service boundaries and control business complexity in an era of high concurrency and prevalent microservices? **DDD (Domain-Driven Design)** has become the ultimate weapon to break the deadlock.

## The Evolution of Software Architecture
To better understand the value of DDD, let's first look at three typical stages in the evolution of software architecture:

* **Traditional Monolithic Architecture**: All application functions are integrated into a single application. All modules and components run within the same process. Requests directly operate on the database, sometimes without even code layering. While easy for early development and deployment, as the business grows, modules with different functions become tightly coupled, and a change in one place affects the whole system.
* **MVC Layered Architecture**: The application is divided into different layers (e.g., controller, service, mapper). Layers interact through interfaces, promoting modularity. However, when faced with complex business scenarios, the flexibility and scalability of the MVC architecture are limited. As the business grows, the service layer can become extremely bloated, and maintenance costs gradually increase.
* **Microservices Architecture**: The system is split into multiple small, independent services. Each service is developed, deployed, and maintained by an independent team. They communicate via lightweight protocols (e.g., HTTP, RPC, or message queues). Services are independent and easy to scale horizontally.

### 🚨 The "Art" Challenge of Microservice Decomposition
With the popularity of microservices, the industry faces a new challenge: **How fine should the granularity of service decomposition be? How should boundaries be defined?**
* **Too Fine Decomposition**: Project complexity is too high, interface network call costs, distributed transaction processing, and service operation and maintenance costs increase significantly.
* **Too Coarse Decomposition**: Business boundaries become blurred, services remain highly coupled, and the advantages of microservices are lost.

**DDD is a methodology that guides us in determining business boundaries based on domain models**, thereby delineating application boundaries, which ultimately translate into service boundaries and code boundaries.

## Core Concepts of DDD (Domain-Driven Design)
DDD uses domain-driven design methods to define domain models, thereby determining business and application boundaries, ensuring consistency between the business model and the code model.

### 1. The Three Main Goals of DDD
* **Implement Business Requirements through Domain Models**: Enable developers and domain experts to jointly understand business requirements, form a shared language, and build models.
* **Improve System Flexibility and Maintainability**: By reasonably dividing bounded contexts, reduce system coupling, allowing different modules to evolve independently.
* **Support the Expression of Complex Business Logic**: Through in-depth business modeling, allow complex business logic to be clearly and accurately reflected in the code.

> **Core Idea**: Make the system more aligned with the business, making large systems easier to build and maintain independently.

### 2. The Two Major Construction Phases of DDD
The construction of DDD is a combination of methods, usually including **Strategic Design** and **Tactical Design**.

#### ① Strategic Design (Business-Oriented: Delineating Boundaries)
Starting from the business, establish domain models and unify bounded contexts. Design usually involves **Event Storming**:
* **Scene Restoration**: Domain experts, architects, developers, testers, product managers, and project managers gather together, brainstorming with colorful sticky notes on a whiteboard.
* **Core Discussion**: What business processes are involved in the system? Which business action triggers another? What are the inputs and outputs?
* **Converging Boundaries**: Sort out the relationships between domain objects, perform clustering, form aggregates, aggregate roots, and bounded contexts, finally completing the decomposition of microservices.

In event storming, product design methods are often combined, such as **Use Case Analysis** (describing system interactions with the outside), **Scenario Analysis** (exploring user usage in different environments), and **User Journey Analysis** (depicting a series of steps a user takes from start to finish).

#### ② Tactical Design (Technology-Oriented: Code Implementation)
Starting from technical implementation, map the domain model to the code model. This phase is mainly responsible for completing code implementation, including the design and implementation of code logic for aggregates, aggregate roots, entities, value objects, etc.

## Hardcore Analysis of DDD System Terminology

To avoid "talking past each other" during actual coding, we need to thoroughly understand the core terminology of DDD:

| Core Term | One-Sentence Definition | Core Features and Code Mapping |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Domain** | The problem space and boundary of business concern. | Used to determine scope and boundaries. Can be further divided into Core Domain (core competitiveness), Generic Domain (e.g., payment), and Supporting Domain (e.g., gateway). |
| **Bounded Context** | The business semantic environment of a unified ubiquitous language. | Defines business boundaries. For example, it's called "Product" in an e-commerce context, but "Cargo" in a shipping context. The context ensures no ambiguity in understanding within the team. |
| **Entity** | A business object with a unique identity (ID). | State is mutable, but the ID remains unchanged. In code, it usually adopts the **Rich Domain Model** (relevant business logic is written directly in the entity class). |
| **Value Object** | An object without a unique identity that only describes characteristics. | State is immutable; modification can only be done by **complete replacement**. For example, a user's "address" attribute. |
| **Aggregate** | A cluster of multiple entities and value objects combined into a whole. | An organization with high cohesion and low coupling. It is the **basic unit** for data modification and persistence, and the smallest unit for microservice decomposition. |
| **Aggregate Root** | The leader within an aggregate, the unified external interface. | The aggregate root is also an entity with an ID. External objects must not directly access other entities within the aggregate; they must cooperate through the interface provided by the aggregate root. |
| **Domain Service** | Cross-object business behavior that cannot be attributed to a single entity. | Encapsulates core business rules. For example, the "order payment" logic involving the interaction of multiple entities like orders, accounts, and payments. |

> 💡 **Seeing the World from a Dynamic Perspective**: Entities and value objects are not static. For a computer host, a graphics card is a value object (replace it with a new one if it breaks); but for the graphics card manufacturer, the card is an entity with a factory serial number that needs to be tracked throughout its lifecycle.

## DDD Architecture Design: Rich Domain Model vs. Anemic Domain Model

When implementing tactical design, how to organize the data and behavior of domain objects is the watershed that distinguishes traditional development from DDD development. This essentially reflects different understandings of the **"division of responsibilities between data and behavior"** in domain objects, mainly divided into the **Anemic Domain Model** and the **Rich Domain Model**.

| Dimension | Anemic Domain Model | Rich Domain Model |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Definition** | Domain objects contain only data (attributes) and getters/setters, with no business logic. | Domain objects contain not only data but also the business logic that processes this data. |
| **Object Role** | A pure "data container" with only state, no behavior. | A true "object-oriented" entity with both state and behavior. |
| **Logic Ownership** | All business logic is stripped away into separate service classes (e.g., Service layer). | Business logic belonging to the object itself is directly cohesive within the object. |
| **Applicable Scenarios** | Suitable for simple business (e.g., traditional CRUD systems). | Suitable for complex business, tightly coupling business logic and data. |

## Four, The Evolution of the Classic DDD Four-Layer Architecture

Having understood the Rich Domain Model, how do we build a stage for these vibrant domain objects? This is the **DDD Four-Layer Layered Architecture Model**. It promotes high cohesion, low coupling, and high maintainability of code by dividing the application into four layers with clear responsibilities.

---

### 1. Hardcore Decomposition of the DDD Four-Layer Architecture



#### ① User Interface Layer
* **Positioning**: Also called the Presentation Layer or Web Layer, mainly responsible for interacting with the outside world (users, APIs of other systems, front-end pages).
* **Responsibilities**: Receives user input and returns system output. **This layer must not contain any business logic**. It is mainly responsible for parameter validation (JSR303), authentication, and forwarding user request data (DTO) to the application layer.

#### ② Application Layer
* **Positioning**: The "diplomat" and "director" of the entire system, mainly used for **coordination and orchestration**.
* **Responsibilities**: The application layer itself does not contain any core business rules. It implements specific business use cases by calling entities/domain services from the domain layer and resources from the infrastructure layer (e.g., sending emails, MQ messages). Additionally, **distributed transaction control (`@Transactional`), security permission checks, and external remote RPC calls** are also handled at this layer.

#### ③ Domain Layer
* **Positioning**: **The absolute core and soul of the entire architecture**, purely cohesive business logic.
* **Responsibilities**: Contains the core business rules, strategies, and state transitions of the application. The previously mentioned aggregate roots, entities, value objects, and domain services all reside here. Its design is completely decoupled from underlying technologies (e.g., databases, caches), aiming solely to accurately translate real business requirements into code.

#### ④ Infrastructure Layer
* **Positioning**: Provides underlying technical support and common services for the upper layers.
* **Responsibilities**: Handles physical interactions with external systems (e.g., MySQL, Redis, RabbitMQ). Provides implementations for data persistence, logging, email sending, and other tools.

---

### 2. Strict Layering vs. Relaxed Layering

In the call relationships of a layered architecture, there are generally two schools of thought in the industry:

* **Strict Layering Architecture**: Each layer can only depend on the **layer directly below it**. That is: `User Interface Layer` -> `Application Layer` -> `Domain Layer` -> `Infrastructure Layer`. This structure is the most robust, with each layer acting as a natural sandbox, achieving the most thorough decoupling.
* **Relaxed Layering Architecture**: The interaction between layers is more flexible. For example, the User Interface Layer or Application Layer can bypass the Domain Layer and directly call the Infrastructure Layer to read underlying report data. This approach is very efficient for rapid development and handling pure query (CQRS) scenarios, but as system complexity increases, it can easily lead to dependency chaos.

> **Byolio Pitfall Avoidance Guide (Dependency Inversion Principle - DIP)**:
> Traditional development habits involve upper layers depending on lower layers, causing the domain layer to be tightly bound to the database.
> DDD's Infrastructure Layer adopts the **Dependency Inversion Principle**. The domain layer only defines the **interface specification** for persistence (e.g., `OrderRepository` interface), while the Infrastructure Layer is responsible for writing the **concrete implementation** of this interface (e.g., `OrderRepositoryImpl`).
> This way, the entire core domain layer becomes a **pure Java module that does not depend on any specific framework**. Even if we later replace MySQL with MongoDB, we only need to rewrite the implementation in the Infrastructure Layer, while the core business logic remains rock-solid.

### 3. Transformation from Traditional Three-Layer Architecture to DDD Four-Layer Architecture

For those familiar with SpringBoot development, we are most accustomed to the three-layer architecture (Controller-Service-Dao). The following diagram and code structure intuitively show how they map and evolve into the DDD four-layer architecture:



#### 📐 Responsibility and Concept Mapping Comparison

| Traditional Three-Layer Architecture (MVC) | DDD Four-Layer Architecture | Core Transformation Logic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Controller Layer** (Presentation Layer) | **User Interface Layer** | Responsibilities are basically aligned, handling HTTP request access, DTO validation, and response assembly. |
| **Service Layer** (Business Logic Layer) | **Application Layer + Domain Layer** | **Core Split Point!** The traditional all-purpose Service is split into two: process orchestration and third-party integration go to `Application`, while core business rules settle into the entities and domain services of `Domain`. |
| **DAO / Repository Layer** | **Infrastructure Layer** | Traditional Dao only writes SQL. DDD's Infrastructure Layer not only handles the database (implementing the Repository interface defined by Domain) but also takes over all technical details like Redis, MQ, etc. |

#### 📁 Evolution of Package Structure in SpringBoot

```text
// Traditional Three-Layer MVC Architecture
com.byolio.project
├── controller     // Various Controllers
├── service        // Bloated business logic, procedural
│   └── impl
├── model          // entity objects / vo objects / dto objects
└── mapper         // MyBatis Mapper

---------------- Evolves into ----------------

// DDD Four-Layer Architecture (Example of Monolithic Module Package Structure)
com.byolio.project
├── interfaces             // 1. User Interface Layer
│   ├── assembler     // DTO converter, responsible for converting request parameters into domain layer entities.
│   ├── controller     // Controller Layer
│   ├── vo             // vo objects
│   └── dto          // Data Transfer Objects, responsible for request parameter validation and response assembly.
|
├── application    // 2. Application Layer (Very thin, only responsible for assembly and orchestration)
│   └── service    // Cross-domain business logic
|
├── domain         // 3. Domain Layer (Core, pure Java, does not depend on specific technical frameworks)
│   ├── order      // Divide bounded contexts by aggregate
│   │   ├── entity      // Rich Domain Model Entity (Aggregate Root)
│   │   ├── valobject    // Value Object
│   │   ├── repository  // Pure interface definition! No implementation
│   │   └── service     // Domain Service
|
├── infrastructure // 4. Infrastructure Layer (Specific technical implementations)
│   ├── config    // Configuration classes
│   ├── common     // Common components
│   ├── api        // API interfaces
│   ├── mapper     // Database mapper layer
│   ├── manager    // Infrastructure common components
│   ├── utils      // Utility classes
│   └── repository // Core! Implements the Repository interface defined in the domain layer
```

> **About the Introduction of `shared` (Shared Domain)**:
> Many beginners of DDD fall into a rigid mindset—since strong isolation is required, each aggregate must be completely independent. This leads to objects like `Money` (amount value object) or `BaseEntity` (base class with common ID and timestamp fields) being rewritten three or four times across packages like `order`, `user`, and `product`.
> The `shared` package is born to break this "rigid isolation." It stores underlying common domain concepts that have reached a high level of consensus across the entire system and are free of business ambiguity.
> However, one must be wary of `shared` becoming a new "big ball of mud"! Only objects without specific business specificity (e.g., common exception definitions, purely generic value objects) should be placed in `shared`. If part of `OrderEntity` is also extracted into `shared` for convenience and directly depended upon by other aggregates, it completely destroys the boundaries of the bounded contexts, leading to total failure. Remember: It's better to have moderate duplication than to blindly share business logic.


## Disadvantages of DDD
Although DDD can perfectly handle complex business, it is by no means a silver bullet. In actual implementation, it also has undeniable pain points:

1. **Extremely Steep Learning Curve**
   DDD introduces a large number of abstract concepts (e.g., bounded contexts, aggregate roots, rich domain models, value objects). Team members need to completely reverse their traditional "CRUD / database table-driven" thinking pattern, which involves a very high cost of mindset transformation.
2. **The Pain Point of Field Modification: "A Change in One Place Affects the Whole System"**
   In traditional "anemic model + database-driven" development, if a new field needs to be added to a table structure, you often only need to modify the underlying Entity and the top-level DTO, and the data can be "transparently passed through."
   However, under DDD's strict layering and anti-corruption mechanisms, due to the introduction of a large number of object isolations, **adding just one field to the underlying table structure may require synchronously modifying 4 or 5 object definitions and their corresponding Mapper converters**. This extremely high cost of change can be very frustrating in the early stages of agile development with frequent changes.
3. **Easily Becomes a Mere Formality, Hollowing Out the Domain Layer (Formalistic DDD)**
   This is the most common "derailment" scenario when domestic teams promote DDD. Due to developers' deep-rooted habits of "procedural programming" and being "Service maniacs," during actual coding, people often prioritize speed, writing all business logic directly in the top-level Application Service, while the lower-level Domain entities and domain services become useless "mouthpieces" and empty shells.
   This formalistic DDD, which has an "external four-layer architecture but an internal anemic core," not only fails to enjoy the benefits of high object cohesion but also adds several layers of unnecessary code calls, becoming a true "architectural burden."

## FAQ
### Why do DDD architectures differ between companies?
This is the most common confusion for students who have just read technical blogs and then look at enterprise-level source code. The answer is simple: **DDD is a design methodology, not a rigid code framework.**

The specific form of the implemented code architecture often depends on the trade-offs and compromises across the following three core dimensions:

#### 1. Team Compromise Between "Strict Layering" and "Engineering Efficiency"
* **Strict Layering**: In some very large financial, payment, or core e-commerce teams, they strictly adhere to the principle of isolation. The UI layer and domain layer might even be in different **Maven Multi-Module** structures. To prevent any contamination, they prefer to write a large number of `Converter`s, with a single field being transformed 4 or 5 times.
* **Engineering Efficiency**: Many small to medium-sized teams or fast-iterating businesses, for the sake of "engineering efficiency," adopt **package isolation within a single module**. They might even allow the `interfaces` layer, in query (CQRS) scenarios, to bypass the domain layer through relaxed layering and directly access the underlying `mapper`. They know that if changing a field requires 5 transformations every time, team development efficiency will be directly dragged down.

#### 2. Different Lifecycle Stages of "Monolith" vs. "Microservices"
* **Monolithic Evolution Stage**: If the system is still a large monolith, a `shared` package is often set up when dividing the package structure. Because within the same process, sharing some basic assets can significantly reduce code duplication.
* **Microservices Maturity Stage**: If the team has already decomposed various aggregates into physically isolated microservices, they will basically remove the large, all-encompassing `shared` package. This is because microservices emphasize **complete decoupling and zero dependencies**. Even if two microservices have 80% similar `Money` value objects, they would rather copy one in each service to gain the ability for independent deployment and evolution.

#### 3. The Influence of Conway's Law: Organizational Structure Determines Code Architecture
> "Organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations." — Conway's Law

Each company has different business complexity and even team personnel structures:
* Some companies have extremely complex and frequently changing businesses (e.g., complex supply chain systems). They will design the `domain` layer to be very thick and heavy, heavily guarded.
* Some companies, although claiming to promote DDD, actually have 90% of their business as statistical reports and CRUD operations. Their `domain` layer will naturally become an empty shell, or the entire architecture will lean more towards the traditional anemic model, just wearing a four-layer skin.

## Summary
DDD is not a specific framework but a design philosophy that brings technology back to the essence of business. Through strategic design, we can clarify complex business boundaries at a macro level; through tactical design, we can use tools like entities and aggregate roots to write more expressive and robust code at a micro level.

When facing long-cycle, cross-departmental collaborative, and business-complex long-term maintenance projects, DDD can endow the system with strong vitality and evolutionary capability.
